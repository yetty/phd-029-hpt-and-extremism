# 1. Core Construct Problem: What Exactly Is “Contamination”?

### Weakness

Your contamination claim is conceptually under-specified.

You assert that ideological congruence may inflate HPT contextualization scores . However, you do not formally distinguish:

- **Construct contamination**
- **Construct overlap**
- **True causal effect of ideology on reasoning**
- **Differential task engagement**

If authoritarian ideology genuinely increases willingness to endorse anti-democratic reasoning consistent with the scenario, this could reflect:

1. Ideological alignment (bias)
2. Accurate contextual modeling (competence)
3. Reduced moral inhibition
4. Greater affective plausibility of extremist actors

Your current model implicitly assumes option (1) but does not rule out (2–4).

**Problem:** Without cognitive-process evidence (e.g., think-alouds), you cannot distinguish ideological projection from disciplined contextualization.

# 2. Measurement Validity of HPT in the Czech Context

You rely heavily on Hartmann & Hasselhorn (2008) .

### Critical issues:

#### 2.1 Dimensional instability in original instrument

Hartmann & Hasselhorn report:

- POP and CONT collapsing into a single factor
- ROA partially unstable
- PCA (not CFA)
- Sample N=170
- 51% explained variance

This is not a strong psychometric foundation.

You assume the CONT subscale is a stable disciplinary construct. The original article does not convincingly establish that.

You should explicitly address:

- Why you assume CONT reflects disciplinary contextualization rather than reverse-coded presentism.
- Whether you confirmed factor structure via CFA in your Czech data before running ideological models.

If you did not preregister factor confirmation, contamination claims are premature.

---

# 3. Ideology Measurement Compression Problem

You reduce FR-LF to 6 items (RD1–3 + NS1–3) , while the full instrument has 18 items across six dimensions .

### Threats:

1. **Construct underrepresentation**
   You exclude xenophobia, antisemitism (partially), social Darwinism dimensions.

2. **Reduced reliability**
   Shortening scales reduces internal consistency and latent precision.

3. **Restricted variance**
   In adolescent Czech samples, endorsement rates are likely extremely low.
   That creates:
   - Floor effects
   - Skewed distributions
   - Attenuated correlations

You must report:

- Distribution plots
- Skewness
- Effective variance
- Reliability (ω, not just α)

If ideological variance is low, null findings are uninterpretable.

---

# 4. Knowledge as Control Variable: Theoretical Ambiguity

You control for knowledge (KN1–KN6) .

But what does knowledge represent?

Possibilities:

1. Domain-specific background schema
2. General academic achievement proxy
3. Teacher grading alignment
4. Ideology-correlated knowledge acquisition

You treat knowledge as a clean covariate.

However:

- Knowledge may mediate ideological effects.
- Knowledge may moderate ideological projection.
- Knowledge may be confounded with school track.

You should test:

- Knowledge × ideology interaction
- School-level random slopes
- Cross-level moderation

Without this, the “net of knowledge” claim is weak.

---

# 5. Multilevel Model Under-Specification

You state multilevel models with clustering by class .

But you do not justify:

- Random intercept only vs random slope
- ICC magnitude
- Between-class ideological variance

Critical question:

If ideological attitudes cluster at class level, contamination could be a **class-level effect**, not individual-level.

You must:

- Decompose ideology into within-class and between-class components.
- Report contextual effects (Mundlak approach).

Otherwise, inference about individual contamination is invalid.

---

# 6. DIF and Measurement Invariance Strategy

You propose DIF and multi-group CFA .

But:

- With N ≈ 300–400, splitting into low/high ideology groups reduces power.
- HPT has only 9 items.
- Likert 1–4 scale reduces discrimination.

Risk:

- Non-significant DIF may be low power, not absence of bias.
- Multi-group CFA with 9 indicators is under-identified if model is unstable.

You need:

- Simulation-based power analysis for DIF detection.
- Report minimum detectable effect size.

Without this, null DIF ≠ measurement invariance.

---

# 7. Social Desirability (SDR-5) Control

You include SDR-5 .

But:

- Social desirability correlates negatively with extremist attitudes.
- It may suppress ideological variance.
- It may correlate with teacher-pleasing contextualization responses.

Controlling for SDR-5 may:

- Remove meaningful ideological signal.
- Introduce collider bias.

You should test models:

1. With SDR
2. Without SDR
3. Ideology × SDR interaction

Otherwise, you cannot claim robustness.

# 8. Theoretical Literature Gaps

You cite motivated cognition generally , but you do not engage with:

- Dual-process models of ideological cognition (Duckitt)
- Authoritarian threat-sensitivity literature
- Moral conviction theory (Skitka)
- Political identity-protective cognition (Kahan)

You assume ideological alignment increases contextual plausibility.

But some research suggests:

- High authoritarians rely on heuristic closure.
- They may show lower integrative complexity.
- They may resist contextual nuance.

Your hypothesis is not theoretically unidirectional.

---

# 9. Inferential Overreach Risk

Your public summary (from the Czech manuscript) concludes:

> HPT is not contaminated and is safe to use.

This is too strong.

Even if contamination is absent:

- This is one scenario (Weimar).
- One cultural context (Czech Republic).
- One age range.
- One instrument translation.

Validity is conditional.

You should frame:

- “Under moderate ideological variance and this task structure…”
- Not general robustness.

TRSE will reject overgeneralization .

---

# 10. Sample Bias

You explicitly note:

- Teachers self-selected.
- Likely modern-history interested.
- Possibly above-average civic orientation.

This creates:

- Restricted ideological variance.
- Normative classroom climates.
- Peer suppression effects.

You cannot treat null results as boundary-robust without quantifying ideological dispersion.

---

# 11. No Process Data

You rely exclusively on rating-scale agreement.

You do not show:

- Justifications
- Open responses
- Cognitive-process indicators

Thus, you measure endorsement, not reasoning quality.

Your claim that CONT represents disciplinary contextualization remains inferential.

---

# 12. Alternative Interpretations (Must Be Addressed Explicitly)

At minimum, discuss:

1. **Floor effect hypothesis**
   Czech students may not endorse NS relativization items strongly enough to create contamination.

2. **Moral inhibition override hypothesis**
   Even authoritarian-leaning students may not express congruence in anonymous survey.

3. **Task constraint hypothesis**
   The HPT scenario structure may constrain acceptable responses sufficiently to block ideological inflation.

4. **Cognitive load hypothesis**
   HPT performance may depend more on inferential integration than ideological sympathy.

If not addressed, reviewers will raise them.

---

# 13. Statistical Power Concerns

With N ≈ 300–400:

- Small ideology effect (β ≈ .10) likely undetectable.
- Cross-level interactions likely underpowered.
- DIF detection low power.

If contamination effect is small but real, your design may not detect it.

You must provide:

- Post hoc detectable effect sizes.
- Confidence intervals, not just p-values.

Pre-registration compliance must be transparent.

# 15. Most Serious Methodological Vulnerabilities (Ranked)

1. Restricted ideological variance (floor effect)
2. Underpowered DIF detection
3. Ambiguous construct separation (contextualization vs congruence)
4. No within-class decomposition
5. Overinterpretation of null result

# 16. What Would Strengthen the Paper

High-leverage additions:

- Report ideology distribution histograms.
- Report ω reliability for all scales.
- Include ideology × knowledge interaction.
- Decompose ideology into within/between class.
- Conduct sensitivity analysis for small effects.
- Explicitly model ceiling/floor effects (ordinal regression).
- Provide CFA confirming 2-factor HPT structure in Czech sample.

---

# 17. Bottom-Line Judgement

**Current risk level for TRSE: Moderate to High.**

Not because the idea is weak — it is strong and original.

But because:

- The null result may be underpowered.
- Construct boundaries are insufficiently specified.
- Alternative explanations are not formally tested.
- Psychometric foundation of HPT is not fully stabilized.

If reframed as:

> A boundary-condition test under restricted ideological variance

— it becomes defensible.

If framed as:

> Demonstrating robustness of HPT against extremism

— it is vulnerable.

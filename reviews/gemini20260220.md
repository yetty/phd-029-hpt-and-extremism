## 1. The Reliability "Red Flag"

The subscale reliabilities are low, particularly for Presentism (POP, = 0.47) and Role of Agent (ROA, = 0.50). In many psychometric circles, an alpha below 0.60 is a non-starter.

- **The Risk:** A reviewer might argue that your null findings for ideology are simply "Type II errors"—you didn't find an effect because your measurements were too noisy to detect one.
- **How to Resolve:** Frame this as a **trade-off between "internal consistency" and "construct representation."** In your discussion, argue that HPT items are intentionally heterogeneous to capture different facets of a complex cognitive process. Use your **McDonald’s Omega** ( total up to 0.69) to argue that total scale reliability is acceptable for research purposes. Crucially, point to the **Knowledge (KN)** effect: if the instrument were "random noise," even knowledge wouldn't predict it so robustly ( = 0.34).

## 2. Low Level-2 (School) Sample Size

You used 10 schools for your random intercepts. Methodologically, at the highest level is right on the edge of what multilevel modeling (MLM) can reliably handle for estimating variance components.

- **The Risk:** A reviewer might claim your Standard Errors are biased or that you lack the power to see school-level ideological "climates."
- **How to Resolve:** Run a **Sensitivity Analysis using Fixed Effects for School** instead of Random Intercepts. If the ideology coefficients remain non-significant in a Fixed Effects model (which doesn't rely on Level-2 sample size assumptions), you can report this in a footnote or the Appendix to prove your results aren't an artifact of the MLM specification.

## 3. The "Moderate" Ideology Problem

Your distributions in **Figure 3** show that few students strongly endorsed right-authoritarianism. Most scores are below the scale midpoint.

- **The Risk:** The "Stress-Test" might be criticized for not being "stressful" enough. If you don't have enough "true believers" in the sample, you haven't really tested if their ideology contaminates the score.
- **How to Resolve:** Re-label the "Stress-Test" as a test of **"Ideological Affinity in a General Population."** In the Limitations section, explicitly state that your results apply to the _range_ of attitudes typically found in a democratic classroom. Suggest that future research should target specific "high-ideology" sub-groups to see if the null finding holds at the extremes.

## 4. Brief Knowledge Assessment

Your primary predictor of success, Historical Knowledge, was measured with only 6 items.

- **The Risk:** It looks like HPT might just be a "reading comprehension" test or a proxy for general intelligence rather than specific historical reasoning.
- **How to Resolve:** In the Method section, emphasize the **"curriculum alignment"** of those 6 items. Explain that they were specifically designed to cover the Weimar Republic context necessary for the HPT scenario. This turns a "short test" into a "highly targeted diagnostic."

## 5. The KSA-3 "Deficit" Signal

You found that higher authoritarianism (KSA-3) predicted _lower_ scores on reversed Presentism ().

- **The Risk:** This contradicts your claim that ideology is "unrelated" to HPT outcomes.
- **How to Resolve:** Embrace this finding. Instead of dismissing it because it wasn't preregistered, use it to bolster your validity argument. Argue that while ideology doesn't **inflate** scores (no "sympathy" effect), certain authoritarian traits might be associated with a **cognitive rigidity** that makes the "unnatural act" of perspective-taking more difficult. This allows you to say: "Ideology doesn't contaminate the test, but it might influence the underlying cognitive ability."

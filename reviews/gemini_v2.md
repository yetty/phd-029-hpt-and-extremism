### 1. The Reliability "Red Flag"

**The Weak Spot:** Your subscale reliabilities are uncomfortably low. You report raw Cronbach's alpha values of 0.47 for Presentism (POP) and 0.50 for Role of Agent (ROA). In many psychometric circles, an alpha below 0.60 will draw immediate reviewer scrutiny. A reviewer might argue that your null findings for ideology are simply "Type II errors"—you didn't find an effect because your measurements were too noisy.

**The Resolution:** \* **Frame it as a trade-off:** In your discussion, explicitly argue that HPT items are intentionally heterogeneous to capture different facets of a complex cognitive process, which naturally depresses Cronbach's alpha (which penalizes heterogeneity).

- **Highlight alternative metrics:** You already smartly included McDonald’s Omega ( total up to 0.69). Emphasize that this is the more appropriate metric for multidimensional constructs.
- **Leverage the KN finding:** Crucially, point to the Historical Knowledge (KN) effect. If the instrument were just "random noise," even knowledge wouldn't predict it so robustly ( for HPT CTX6). The fact that KN pierces through the noise proves the instrument carries a genuine signal.

### 2. Low Level-2 (School) Sample Size

**The Weak Spot:** You utilized multilevel models (MLM) with 10 schools and 20 classrooms as random intercepts. Methodologically, 10 clusters at the highest level is right on the edge of what MLM can reliably handle for estimating variance components. Reviewers might claim your Standard Errors are biased.

**The Resolution:**

- **Add a Fixed-Effects Sensitivity Check:** You already have an excellent "Sensitivity Analyses" section. Add one more line to it: state that you ran a sensitivity analysis using Fixed Effects for schools (dummy variables) instead of Random Intercepts. Fixed-effects models do not rely on Level-2 sample size assumptions. If the ideology coefficients remain non-significant in this model, you can definitively state that the null result is not an artifact of MLM specifications.

### 3. The "Moderate" Ideology Problem

**The Weak Spot:** Your distributions in Figure 3 show that very few students strongly endorsed right-authoritarianism. Both FR-LF (_M_ = 2.48) and KSA-3 (_M_ = 2.86) scores are positively skewed and sit below the scale midpoint of 3. A reviewer could argue that you didn't truly "stress-test" the instrument because you didn't have enough highly ideological students to cause contamination.

**The Resolution:**

- **Clarify the boundary conditions:** Re-label the "Stress-Test" concept in your limitations section as a test of "Ideological Affinity in a General Population."
- **Own the limitation:** Explicitly state that your results apply to the range of attitudes typically found in a mainstream democratic classroom. Suggest that future research should use targeted sampling to locate specific "high-ideology" sub-groups to see if the null finding holds at the extremes.

### 4. The Brief Knowledge Assessment

**The Weak Spot:** Your primary predictor of HPT success, Historical Knowledge (KN), was measured with only a 6-item multiple-choice test. A reviewer might argue this is too blunt an instrument to capture nuanced historical understanding, making HPT look more like a proxy for general reading comprehension.

**The Resolution:**

- **Emphasize Curriculum Alignment:** In the Method section, heavily emphasize the _targeted_ nature of these 6 items. Explain that they were carefully designed to cover the exact Weimar Republic context necessary for the HPT scenario, aligning with the Czech ninth-grade curriculum. This reframes a "short test" into a "highly targeted diagnostic" of scenario-relevant background knowledge.

### 5. The KSA-3 "Deficit" Signal

**The Weak Spot:** You actually _did_ find a statistically significant ideology effect: a negative association between KSA-3 (authoritarianism) and POP_rev (). Dismissing this simply because it was "not preregistered" might look like sweeping inconvenient data under the rug.

**The Resolution:**

- **Embrace the "Cognitive Rigidity" narrative:** Use this finding to bolster your validity argument. As you briefly noted in the text, point out that while ideology doesn't _inflate_ scores (no "sympathy" effect), authoritarian traits are heavily associated with cognitive rigidity. Argue that the "unnatural act" of perspective-taking is genuinely more difficult for highly authoritarian students. This allows you to assert: "Ideology doesn't unfairly contaminate the test via sympathy, but authoritarian cognitive styles may genuinely inhibit the complex reasoning required."

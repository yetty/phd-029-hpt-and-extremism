Scenario-based instruments are widely used to assess complex reasoning in psychology and education. Existing measurement frameworks address item bias and structural equivalence, but no integrated protocol targets the specific threat that attitudinally charged content introduces construct-irrelevant variance through attitude-congruence effects. I submit the preprint "Cross-Cultural Validation and Ideological Fairness of a Historical Perspective Taking Instrument: Evidence from Czech Secondary Students" for evaluation under the Quantitative Psychology and Educational Psychology thematic fields.

The study tests whether a politically charged historical scenario introduces construct-irrelevant variance — score variation caused by factors other than the target skill — when students' ideological attitudes align with the scenario's content. Using the first Czech adaptation of the Hartmann and Hasselhorn Historical Perspective Taking instrument, a scenario-based questionnaire in which students reason about a historical actor's decisions (N = 293 secondary students, 10 schools), I examined whether students with stronger right-authoritarian attitudes score higher simply because the scenario resonates with their beliefs rather than because they reason historically.

Three converging analyses found no evidence of such bias. Differential item functioning analysis found no items behaving differently across ideology groups. Multi-group confirmatory factor analysis confirmed that the instrument measures the same construct on the same scale across groups (full scalar invariance by conventional fit-index criteria). Multilevel models showed that ideology was unrelated to scores after controlling for historical knowledge and social desirability. Historical knowledge was the strongest correlate of performance, consistent with the known knowledge-dependence of disciplinary reasoning; the cross-sectional design precludes causal inference about this relationship.

Beyond the domain-specific validation, the paper synthesises five individually preregistered analyses into a protocol for evaluating whether scenario-based instrument content generates construct-irrelevant variance through attitude-congruence effects. Rather than treating ideological attitudes as a generic covariate, the protocol tests each mechanism separately: item-level bias (DIF), structural equivalence (multi-group CFA), regression effects (multilevel models), and practical significance (equivalence testing, which provides positive evidence that effects fall within a pre-specified equivalence bound). This combination is applicable to domains where rich contextual material elicits complex reasoning but may interact with respondents' prior beliefs — moral reasoning, civic education, and science literacy are immediate candidates, provided adequate sample sizes and comparable item formats.

I preregistered this study on OSF before data collection began. All data, analysis scripts, instruments, and a reproducibility report are publicly available at https://doi.org/10.17605/OSF.IO/YNG37. A Transparency and Openness Promotion (TOP) disclosure table is appended to the preprint.

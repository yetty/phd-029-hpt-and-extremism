## Abstract

Cross-cultural validation of educational instruments is essential for ensuring that scores carry equivalent meaning across linguistic and curricular contexts. This study reports the first Czech adaptation and psychometric evaluation of the Hartmann and Hasselhorn Historical Perspective Taking (HPT) instrument, a scenario-based measure widely used in European history education research. Because the instrument's scenario involves a young man in the Weimar Republic contemplating joining an extremist movement, we additionally examined whether students' political attitudes introduce construct-irrelevant variance---an ideological fairness concern. We administered the Czech HPT adaptation alongside measures of right-authoritarian ideology, authoritarianism, historical knowledge, and social desirability to 293 secondary students (20 classrooms, 10 schools). Confirmatory factor analysis supported the theorized three-factor structure (CFI = .999, RMSEA = .011). Subscale reliability was modest but consistent with expectations for three-item scales ($\alpha$ = .47--.64; $\omega$ = .55--.69). IRT-based differential item functioning analysis detected no biased items across ideology groups (0/9 flagged). Multi-group CFA demonstrated full scalar measurement invariance ($\Delta$CFI $\leq$ .003, $\Delta$RMSEA $\leq$ .006). Historical knowledge was the only significant predictor of HPT scores ($\beta$ = 0.21--0.34), while ideology was unrelated ($\beta$ $\approx$ 0). These findings provide initial evidence supporting ideological fairness and convergent validity for the Czech adaptation, suggesting that structured scenario-based assessments using politically charged content can function fairly across ideological subgroups.

**Keywords:** historical perspective taking, cross-cultural validation, measurement invariance, differential item functioning, construct-irrelevant variance, test fairness

## 1. Introduction

The international expansion of history education research has produced growing demand for validated instruments that measure disciplinary reasoning competences across languages and curricula. Historical perspective taking (HPT)---the capacity to reconstruct the beliefs, values, and circumstances that made past actors' decisions intelligible in their own time [@Lee2001; @Hartmann2008]---is recognized across national frameworks as a core component of historical literacy [@Seixas2013; @vanDrie2007; @Wineburg2001]. Yet the measurement of HPT across cultural and linguistic boundaries remains underdeveloped. While several HPT instruments exist, few have undergone the systematic cross-cultural validation that educational measurement standards require [@AERA2014].

The instrument developed by @Hartmann2008 represents the most widely used standardized HPT measure in European research. Originally validated with German secondary students, it has been adapted for use in the Netherlands [@Huijgen2014; @Huijgen2017] and employed in comparative studies of historical reasoning. The instrument operationalizes HPT through a scenario-based approach in which students read about a historically situated dilemma and respond to nine Likert-scaled items capturing three developmental levels: presentism (POP), recognition of the agent's role (ROA), and contextualisation (CONT). This structure reflects a theoretical progression from naive present-centered judgment toward increasingly sophisticated historical reasoning [@Hartmann2008; @Lee2001].

The present study reports the first Czech adaptation and psychometric evaluation of the Hartmann and Hasselhorn HPT instrument. Two considerations motivate this work. First, no validated Czech-language HPT instrument currently exists, despite growing interest in historical thinking assessment within Czech education reform [@Cinatl2021; @Munich2023; @Ripka2024]. The Czech adaptation addresses this gap and provides the research community with a tool for cross-cultural comparisons of HPT development.

Second, the instrument's scenario content raises a specific fairness question. The scenario describes a young man in the Weimar Republic deliberating whether to join an extremist political movement. Items that require students to contextualize this figure's reasoning could, in principle, function differently for students whose political attitudes align with the scenario's ideological content. Students with right-authoritarian sympathies might find it cognitively easier to endorse contextualizing responses---not because they are reasoning historically, but because the responses resonate with their own attitudes. If present, such a pattern would constitute construct-irrelevant variance (CIV): systematic score variation attributable to a factor extraneous to the target construct [@Messick1995; @AERA2014].

This concern is not merely theoretical. Research on identity-protective cognition demonstrates that individuals process information through the lens of pre-existing beliefs [@Kahan2017; @Jost2003]. Recent work has documented how ideological content can infiltrate competence judgments in history assessment, whether through teacher scoring bias [@Alven2019] or through construct comparability challenges in cross-cultural settings [@Badham2025]. For a measurement audience, the question is whether the HPT instrument's psychometric properties---its factor structure, item parameters, and score interpretations---hold equivalently across students with different political attitudes, or whether politically charged content introduces measurement bias.

We address this question through a comprehensive validation study that integrates confirmatory factor analysis (CFA), reliability analysis, intraclass correlation estimation, IRT-based differential item functioning (DIF) analysis, and multi-group CFA for measurement invariance testing. The study contributes to the measurement literature in three ways: (a) it provides the first published Czech validation of the Hartmann and Hasselhorn HPT instrument, (b) it offers DIF and invariance evidence for a fairness-relevant grouping variable---political ideology---that has not previously been examined in HPT measurement, and (c) it demonstrates a methodological approach for evaluating whether politically charged assessment content introduces construct-irrelevant variance.

## 2. Literature Review

### 2.1 Historical Perspective Taking and Its Measurement

HPT refers to the ability to understand past actors' decisions by reconstructing the historical context in which they operated [@Lee2001; @Hartmann2008]. The concept has roots in British traditions distinguishing rational reconstruction from naive presentism [@Ashby1987; @Lee2001] and has been situated within broader frameworks of historical reasoning that encompass evidence use, argumentation, and substantive concept deployment [@VanBoxtel2018; @vanDrie2007]. Across these frameworks, HPT is understood as a cognitively demanding, knowledge-dependent competence---not a disposition but a disciplinary achievement requiring both domain-specific knowledge and the capacity to deploy that knowledge in reconstructing past perspectives [@Chapman2021; @Wineburg2001].

@Hartmann2008 operationalized HPT into a developmental model with three levels: *presentism* (POP), where students judge historical actors by contemporary standards; *recognition of the agent's role* (ROA), where students acknowledge the agent's social position without full contextual reconstruction; and *contextualisation* (CONT), where students situate decisions within the specific historical conditions that rendered them intelligible. The original validation study (*n* = 170 German students, Grade 10) recovered three levels through principal component analysis (explained variance = 51%), and students classified at the highest level had significantly better history grades. The instrument was extended by @Huijgen2014 and @Huijgen2017, who confirmed the core theoretical structure while highlighting the difficulty of contextualisation for most students.

The difficulty of measuring HPT stems from its inherently interpretive character. Unlike factual recall, HPT cannot be assessed through simple multiple-choice items with single correct answers [@Smith2017; @Breakstone2014]. Scenario-based instruments address this challenge by presenting contextual information and requiring students to evaluate statements reflecting different levels of historical reasoning [@Hartmann2008; @Seixas2015b]. However, this approach introduces a measurement tension: the richer the historical context, the more opportunity for construct-irrelevant factors---including attitudes toward that content---to influence responses.

### 2.2 Cross-Cultural Test Adaptation

The adaptation of educational and psychological instruments across languages and cultures requires systematic procedures to ensure that the adapted version measures the same construct as the original [@ITC2017; @Hambleton2005]. The International Test Commission (ITC) guidelines emphasize that adaptation involves more than translation: it requires attention to construct equivalence, cultural appropriateness, and technical quality of the adapted version [@ITC2017]. For instruments used in history education, cross-cultural adaptation faces the additional challenge that historical content may carry different resonances across national contexts. A scenario about the Weimar Republic, originally designed for German students, intersects differently with Czech historical memory---where the period connects directly to the 1938 Munich Agreement, the cession of the Sudetenland, and the Nazi occupation of 1939--1945.

Cross-cultural validation studies should demonstrate that the adapted instrument preserves the factor structure, reliability, and validity relationships of the original [@Hambleton2005]. At minimum, this requires confirming the theorized factor structure through CFA, reporting reliability estimates appropriate for the item characteristics, and providing convergent validity evidence through expected correlations with related constructs. When the instrument will be used to compare groups or predict outcomes, measurement invariance testing becomes essential [@AERA2014].

### 2.3 Construct-Irrelevant Variance in Educational Assessment

The unified validity framework identifies construct-irrelevant variance as a principal threat to score interpretation [@Messick1995; @AERA2014]. CIV occurs when systematic variance in test scores is attributable to factors extraneous to the target construct. When assessment content intersects with respondents' attitudes or social identities, CIV can arise through attitude-consistent responding: students whose beliefs align with test content may find certain response options more natural or appealing, inflating their scores without corresponding gains in the assessed competence [@Messick1995].

For the HPT instrument specifically, the concern is that the Weimar Republic scenario creates a *congruence pathway*: students with right-authoritarian attitudes might endorse contextualizing statements about the historical figure's attraction to extremism not through disciplinary reasoning but through ideological sympathy. This would represent construct contamination in @Messick1995's terms---a systematic source of score variance unrelated to HPT competence. Evidence of internal structure, including measurement invariance across relevant subgroups, constitutes essential validity evidence when scores are used to compare groups or when the construct definition requires demonstrating that extraneous factors do not influence scores [@AERA2014].

An alternative theoretical prediction deserves mention. Research on authoritarian cognition suggests that high authoritarianism is associated with reduced integrative complexity---the capacity to hold multiple conflicting perspectives simultaneously [@Suedfeld1977; @Duckitt2001]. Because HPT requires precisely this kind of integrative capacity, authoritarian students might perform *worse*, not better. Additionally, dual-process theories of cognition [@Evans2013] suggest that structured assessment formats may channel deliberative processing, overriding the automatic attitude-retrieval processes that would produce attitude-consistent responding. If so, the assessment format itself may buffer against attitudinal interference.

### 2.4 DIF and Measurement Invariance as Fairness Evidence

Differential item functioning and measurement invariance testing provide complementary approaches to evaluating measurement fairness [@Chen2007; @Cheung2002; @Finch2005]. DIF analysis examines whether individual items function differently across groups after controlling for the latent trait, identifying specific items that may be biased. Measurement invariance testing through multi-group CFA examines whether the overall measurement model---including factor structure (configural invariance), factor loadings (metric invariance), and item thresholds or intercepts (scalar invariance)---holds across groups.

For ordinal items, the weighted least squares mean and variance adjusted (WLSMV) estimator is recommended for CFA [@Flora2004], and threshold-based invariance testing replaces intercept-based approaches. The conventional criteria for evaluating invariance steps use changes in fit indices rather than chi-square difference tests, which are sensitive to sample size: $\Delta$CFI $\leq$ .01 and $\Delta$RMSEA $\leq$ .015 indicate that the more constrained model does not fit meaningfully worse [@Chen2007; @Cheung2002].

In the context of HPT measurement, establishing scalar invariance across ideology groups would mean that the factor structure, loadings, and item thresholds are equivalent for students with different political attitudes---a necessary condition for concluding that observed score differences (or the lack thereof) reflect true construct differences rather than measurement artifacts.

### 2.5 The Present Study

This study addresses two interrelated measurement questions:

1. Does the Czech adaptation of the Hartmann and Hasselhorn HPT instrument demonstrate acceptable psychometric properties, including the theorized three-factor structure, adequate reliability, and convergent validity with historical knowledge?

2. Does the instrument function fairly across students with different political attitudes, as evidenced by the absence of DIF and by scalar measurement invariance across ideology groups?

We hypothesized that the three-factor structure would be confirmed and that the instrument would demonstrate measurement invariance across ideology groups. As convergent validity evidence, we expected historical knowledge to correlate positively with HPT scores, consistent with the theoretical conceptualization of HPT as a knowledge-dependent competence. A companion paper reports the full substantive analysis of ideology-HPT relationships using multilevel modeling (Author, in preparation for *Social Psychology of Education*); the present paper focuses on the measurement properties and fairness evidence.

## 3. Method

### 3.1 Adaptation Process

The Czech HPT instrument was developed through a systematic translation and adaptation process following ITC guidelines [@ITC2017]. Two bilingual Czech-English experts conducted independent forward translations from the published English version of the instrument. An independent backward translation was produced by a third bilingual expert unfamiliar with the original. Five Czech history educators at the secondary level reviewed the adapted items for conceptual equivalence, age-appropriateness, and curricular alignment. No items required substantive modification; minor wording adjustments were made to two items for naturalness in Czech. Reviewers confirmed that the Weimar Republic scenario content was familiar to Czech secondary students through the national history curriculum, given the direct connections between the interwar period and subsequent Czech historical experience (the Munich Agreement, the cession of the Sudetenland, and the Nazi occupation).

The adaptation preserved the original instrument's structure: the four-point response format (1 = *strongly disagree* to 4 = *strongly agree*), the three-factor item structure (three items per subscale), and the reverse-scoring of POP items were retained unchanged from the validated German version. All deviations from the original were documented transparently in the OSF repository (https://osf.io/yng37/). The scenario narrative was cross-checked against the Czech history curriculum to confirm that all contextual references were accessible to the target population.

No cognitive pilot study (e.g., think-aloud interviews) was conducted prior to main data collection, which constitutes a limitation of the adaptation process (see Section 5.9).

### 3.2 Participants

The sample comprised 293 students from 20 classrooms across 10 schools in the Czech Republic, spanning lower-secondary (ISCED 2; *n* = 87, 29.7%) and upper-secondary (ISCED 3; *n* = 206, 70.3%) levels. The inclusion criterion was completion of the curricular unit on the interwar period. Schools were drawn from five Czech regions (Praha: 42.7%; Kralovehradecky: 34.8%; Zlinsky: 11.3%; Jihomoravsky: 8.2%; Ustecky: 3.1%) and three funding categories (public: 90.8%; private: 6.5%; church-affiliated: 2.7%). Gender composition was 192 male (65.5%), 88 female (30.0%), and 13 identifying as another gender (4.4%). Students' most recent history grade averaged 1.71 (*SD* = 0.92; Czech scale: 1 = excellent, 5 = failing).

Schools were recruited through convenience sampling; the sample over-represents upper-secondary students relative to the national age cohort composition and should not be treated as a probability sample of Czech adolescents. Sample size was determined by an a priori power analysis specified in the preregistered protocol (OSF: https://osf.io/yng37/), targeting 80% power to detect standardized ideology effects of $\beta$ $\geq$ .15 in a two-level multilevel model; the achieved *N* = 285--293 met this target. Within each school, all students present on the day of administration completed the instrument battery during a regular class period (approximately 30--40 minutes). Teachers were present during administration but did not intervene in students' responses.

### 3.3 Instruments

#### 3.3.1 Historical Perspective Taking (HPT)

The Czech adaptation of the @Hartmann2008 scenario-based HPT instrument. Students read a narrative about a young man in the Weimar Republic considering joining an extremist political movement, then responded to nine items on a 4-point Likert scale. Three items each tapped presentism (POP1--POP3), recognition of the agent's role (ROA1--ROA3), and contextualisation (CONT1--CONT3). POP items capture present-centered moral judgments of the historical figure (e.g., evaluating his decision by contemporary standards); these are reverse-scored (5 $-$ POP) so that higher scores indicate more contextualised reasoning. ROA items reflect awareness of the agent's social position and circumstances without full contextual integration. CONT items represent the most sophisticated level, requiring students to situate the historical figure's deliberation within the specific political, economic, and social conditions of the Weimar Republic. The primary composite was HPT_CTX6, the mean of reversed POP and CONT items. HPT_TOT9 (adding ROA) served as a secondary outcome.

#### 3.3.2 Right-Authoritarian Ideology (FR-LF mini)

A six-item short form of the Fragebogen Rechtsextremer Einstellungen---Leipziger Form [@Decker2013], comprising acceptance of right-wing dictatorship (RD; 3 items) and National Socialist sympathy/relativisation (NS; 3 items) facets, rated on a 5-point scale. The NS facet is theoretically the most direct operationalisation of potential content congruence with the Weimar scenario.

#### 3.3.3 Authoritarianism (KSA-3)

The nine-item Kurzskala Autoritarismus [@Beierlein2014], measuring authoritarian aggression, submission, and conventionalism on a 5-point scale. This instrument was used for a supplementary measurement invariance check using an alternative ideology operationalization.

#### 3.3.4 Historical Knowledge (KN)

Six multiple-choice items covering content relevant to the HPT scenario, scored dichotomously (sum score 0--6). This measure served as a convergent validity criterion, given the theoretical expectation that HPT is a knowledge-dependent competence.

#### 3.3.5 Social Desirability (SDR-5)

The five-item short form [@Hays1989], rated on a 5-point scale with items SDR2--SDR4 reverse-coded. Included to evaluate whether HPT scores reflect impression management rather than genuine reasoning.

### 3.4 Analytic Approach

All analyses were conducted in R (version 4.4.2) using lavaan for CFA, mirt for IRT-based DIF analysis, psych for reliability estimation, and lme4/performance for intraclass correlations.

**Factor structure.** CFA compared one-factor (general HPT), two-factor (POP+CONT vs. ROA), and three-factor (POP, ROA, CONT) models using the WLSMV estimator, appropriate for ordinal item responses [@Flora2004]. Model evaluation used CFI ($\geq$ .95 acceptable, $\geq$ .97 good), TLI ($\geq$ .95), RMSEA ($\leq$ .08 acceptable, $\leq$ .05 good), and SRMR ($\leq$ .08).

**Reliability.** Internal consistency was assessed via Cronbach's $\alpha$ (raw and based on polychoric correlations) and McDonald's $\omega$, which does not assume tau-equivalence and is generally preferred for congeneric measures [@Flora2004].

**Intraclass correlations.** ICCs at the school and classroom levels were computed to characterize the multilevel structure of the data and to evaluate whether clustering warranted multilevel approaches.

**Differential item functioning.** Low and High ideology groups were formed using tertile splits on a composite ideology score (mean of *z*-scored FR-LF and KSA-3), with the middle tertile excluded to maximize contrast (*n* $\approx$ 96 per group). This extreme-group design follows established practice in DIF research [@Finch2005]. A constrained multi-group graded response model tested each item for uniform (threshold differences) and non-uniform (slope differences) DIF with Bonferroni correction ($\alpha$ = .01).

**Multi-group CFA.** Stepwise MG-CFA tested configural, metric, and scalar invariance across ideology groups using the three-factor model with WLSMV estimation. Invariance was evaluated using $\Delta$CFI $\leq$ .01 and $\Delta$RMSEA $\leq$ .015 criteria [@Chen2007; @Cheung2002].

**Convergent validity.** Pearson correlations between HPT composites and historical knowledge provided convergent validity evidence.

**Supplementary invariance check.** To verify that invariance findings were not an artifact of the composite ideology definition, MG-CFA was also conducted using NS-only grouping (the facet most directly content-congruent with the Weimar scenario).

## 4. Results

### 4.1 Factor Structure

CFA supported the three-factor model (POP, ROA, CONT) as best-fitting, outperforming the two-factor and one-factor alternatives (Table 1). The three-factor model showed excellent global fit: CFI = .999, TLI = .998, RMSEA = .011 (90% CI [.000, .051]), SRMR = .048. The two-factor model combining POP and CONT items on a single factor showed acceptable but inferior fit (CFI = .956, RMSEA = .057), while the one-factor model fit poorly (CFI = .926, RMSEA = .073).

|-------|----------|------|-----|-----|-------|--------|------|
| 1-Factor | 66.68 | 27 | .926 | .902 | .073 | [.051, .095] | .075 |
| 2-Factor (POP+CONT, ROA) | 49.48 | 26 | .956 | .940 | .057 | [.032, .081] | .066 |

*Note.* WLSMV = weighted least squares mean and variance adjusted. All items treated as ordered categorical. The 2-factor model groups POP and CONT items on a single factor with ROA on a separate factor. Chi-square values are mean-and-variance adjusted (WLSMV). Nested model comparisons require the DIFFTEST procedure rather than standard chi-square difference tests. All *p* < .001 except the three-factor model (*p* = .418).

The near-perfect global fit of the three-factor model reflects the small item set (nine items in three just-identified three-indicator factors) and should be interpreted alongside local fit diagnostics: the largest modification index was 7.35 (below the threshold of 10), and standardized residual correlations were small ($|r|$ $\leq$ .13). These results confirm that the Czech adaptation preserves the theorized three-level structure of the original German instrument.

Inter-factor correlations from the three-factor CFA were: POP--ROA $\Phi$ = .377, POP--CONT $\Phi$ = .509, ROA--CONT $\Phi$ = .631. These moderate-to-strong correlations indicate meaningful associations among the HPT dimensions, with ROA and CONT showing the strongest link. Discriminant validity is supported since all inter-factor correlations are below .85, confirming that the three subscales capture related but distinct aspects of historical perspective taking.

A bifactor model specifying one general HPT factor and three specific residual factors was also tested ($\chi^2$ = 14.70, *df* = 18, *p* = .682, CFI = 1.000, TLI = 1.012, RMSEA = .000 [.000, .043], SRMR = .037). General factor loadings ranged from .116 (POP2) to .632 (CONT1), with CONT items loading highest on the general factor. POP and ROA specific factors retained meaningful loadings (.28--.66), while CONT-specific loadings were non-significant. The bifactor model fit well but did not meaningfully improve over the correlated three-factor model ($\Delta$CFI = .001, $\Delta$RMSEA = .011). General factor loadings were strongest for CONT items, while POP and ROA retained meaningful specific factor variance. This pattern suggests that while a general HPT dimension exists, the subscale-level structure captures meaningful additional variance, supporting subscale-level interpretation for POP and ROA.

Table 3 presents reliability coefficients for each HPT subscale. Raw Cronbach's $\alpha$ values ranged from .47 (POP) to .64 (CONT); polychoric $\alpha$ (appropriate for ordinal items) ranged from .53 to .69; McDonald's $\omega$ ranged from .55 to .69. CONT demonstrated the strongest reliability, consistent with its highest average factor loadings.

*Note.* POP items are reverse-scored. $\alpha$ = Cronbach's alpha; $\omega$ = McDonald's omega total. Mean $r_{ii}$ = mean inter-item correlation.

These reliability values are modest by conventional standards but must be interpreted in context. Three considerations are relevant. First, reliability is a direct function of the number of items, and with only three items per subscale, $\alpha$ is structurally constrained. With *k* = 3 items and assuming moderate mean inter-item correlations in the range .20--.35 (typical for short educational assessment subscales), $\alpha$ values of .47--.64 are consistent with the Spearman-Brown prediction; extending each subscale to six items would yield projected $\alpha$ values of .64--.79. Second, $\omega$ is more appropriate than $\alpha$ for congeneric measures where items have unequal loadings, as is clearly the case here (Table 2). The $\omega$ values (.55--.69) are consistently higher than raw $\alpha$, reflecting the congeneric nature of the subscales. Third, the original @Hartmann2008 validation did not report $\alpha$ per subscale but noted that the three-factor solution accounted for 51% of variance, suggesting comparable internal consistency in the German original. The Dutch adaptation by @Huijgen2014 similarly worked with short subscales and reported reliability challenges inherent to the instrument's design.

Critically, modest subscale reliability does not prevent the detection of valid predictor relationships. The strong and consistent knowledge--HPT correlations reported below ($r$ = .22--.34 across subscales of varying reliability) demonstrate that the instrument carries genuine psychometric signal. Furthermore, correcting the observed FR-LF--HPT_CTX6 correlation ($r$ = -.043) for attenuation due to unreliability yielded a disattenuated $r$ = -.065, indicating that measurement error does not plausibly mask a meaningful ideology--HPT association.

### 4.4 Intraclass Correlations

Intraclass correlations indicated meaningful school-level clustering for most HPT outcomes (HPT_CTX6: ICC_school = .041; HPT_TOT9: ICC_school = .085; ROA: ICC_school = .102), consistent with between-school differences in instructional emphasis and student composition. Classroom-level variance was near zero for the primary composite but non-negligible for CONT (ICC_class = .033) and ROA (ICC_class = .104). These ICCs justify multilevel modeling for substantive analyses and indicate that the instrument is sensitive to contextual variation in historical reasoning opportunities---a desirable property for a measure intended to capture educationally meaningful differences.

### 4.6 Measurement Invariance

Table 5 presents the stepwise MG-CFA results. The configural model demonstrated adequate fit (CFI = .978, RMSEA = .055, SRMR = .081), confirming that the three-factor structure holds separately in both ideology groups. Constraining factor loadings to equality across groups (metric invariance) produced no meaningful deterioration ($\Delta$CFI = .000, $\Delta$RMSEA = $-$.006). Constraining both loadings and thresholds (scalar invariance) similarly maintained fit ($\Delta$CFI = +.002, $\Delta$RMSEA = $-$.007). Both transitions satisfy the established criteria of $|\Delta$CFI$|$ $\leq$ .01 and $|\Delta$RMSEA$|$ $\leq$ .015 [@Chen2007; @Cheung2002], providing evidence for full scalar invariance.

**Table 5.** *Multi-Group CFA Fit Indices Across Invariance Levels (WLSMV)*

| Model | CFI | RMSEA | SRMR | $\Delta$CFI | $\Delta$RMSEA |
|-------|-----|-------|------|-------------|---------------|
| Configural | .978 | .055 | .081 | --- | --- |
| Metric | .978 | .049 | .084 | .000 | $-$.006 |
| Scalar | .980 | .042 | .082 | +.002 | $-$.007 |

*Note.* $\Delta$CFI and $\Delta$RMSEA computed relative to the preceding model. Invariance criteria: $|\Delta$CFI$|$ $\leq$ .01, $|\Delta$RMSEA$|$ $\leq$ .015 [@Chen2007]. Low and High ideology groups formed by tertile split on composite ideology (*n* $\approx$ 96 per group). Items treated as ordered categorical. A lavaan warning about negative estimated observed variances occurred for some models, which can occur with small group sizes and WLSMV estimation; this does not invalidate the invariance conclusions but is noted for transparency.

The decrease in RMSEA from configural (.055) to scalar (.042) reflects the parsimony correction inherent in the RMSEA formula: as equality constraints are imposed and the model degrees of freedom increase, the penalty for model complexity decreases. This pattern is expected when the imposed constraints are substantively appropriate.

Full scalar invariance means that the factor structure, factor loadings, and item thresholds are equivalent for students with low and high ideological attitudes. This is the strongest level of invariance commonly tested and is a necessary condition for meaningful group comparisons. In substantive terms, it means that any observed score differences between ideology groups can be attributed to true differences in HPT competence rather than to measurement artifacts---and that the absence of such differences (as found in this study) reflects genuine equivalence in HPT performance.

### 4.7 Supplementary Invariance: NS-Only Grouping

To verify that invariance findings were not an artifact of the composite ideology grouping variable, we repeated the MG-CFA using groups defined by the NS facet alone (National Socialist sympathy/relativisation), which represents the most direct theoretical operationalization of content congruence with the Weimar scenario. The supplementary analysis confirmed scalar invariance (scalar $\Delta$CFI = +.002), indicating that the instrument functions equivalently even when groups are defined by the ideology facet most proximal to the scenario content.

### 4.8 Convergent Validity

Historical knowledge correlated positively with all HPT outcomes, providing convergent validity evidence consistent with the theoretical expectation that HPT is a knowledge-dependent competence: *r* = .34 (HPT_CTX6), *r* = .22 (CONT), *r* = .32 (POP_rev). These moderate correlations indicate that students with stronger knowledge of the relevant historical period scored higher on HPT, while also demonstrating that HPT is not reducible to knowledge alone---the shared variance (approximately 5--12%) leaves substantial HPT variance attributable to reasoning processes beyond factual recall.

History grade (*M* = 1.71, *SD* = 0.92, Czech scale 1 = excellent to 5 = failing) provides an additional source of criterion validity evidence. Negative correlations reflect the Czech grading scale (1 = excellent, 5 = failing). The primary composite HPT_CTX6 correlated significantly with history grade (*r* = $-$.153, *p* = .010), providing additional criterion validity evidence: students with better grades scored higher on HPT. POP_rev showed the strongest association (*r* = $-$.178, *p* = .003), followed by the full composite HPT_TOT9 (*r* = $-$.130, *p* = .029). CONT (*r* = $-$.072, *p* = .227) and ROA (*r* = $-$.036, *p* = .541) did not reach significance individually, consistent with their narrower content scope and the reduced power of three-item subscale scores.

By contrast, ideology measures showed negligible correlations with HPT (FR-LF--HPT_CTX6: *r* = $-$.05; FR-LF--CONT: *r* = .01; FR-LF--POP_rev: *r* = $-$.09). Social desirability was also unrelated (*r* = $-$.02 to .01). This pattern---significant knowledge correlations, null ideology correlations, null social desirability correlations---is precisely what would be expected if the instrument measures disciplinary reasoning rather than some combination of attitudes and impression management.

### 4.10 Substantive Context: Ideology-HPT Relationships

This study shares its dataset with a companion paper reporting the substantive analysis of ideology--HPT relationships (Author, in preparation for *Social Psychology of Education*). The two papers address distinct research questions---measurement validation versus substantive predictive relationships---and report non-overlapping primary analyses. For context, ideology was unrelated to HPT scores across all model specifications: FR-LF $\beta$ = 0.012, *SE* = 0.067, 95% CI [$-$0.120, 0.143], *p* = .863 for the primary outcome (HPT_CTX6). Historical knowledge was the only consistent predictor ($\beta$ = 0.21--0.34, *p* < .001). Post hoc equivalence tests (TOST; SESOI = $\pm$ 0.20) confirmed that ideology effects are statistically ruled out as practically significant (all TOST *p*s < .003). The ideology coefficient remained non-significant across all sensitivity checks, including alternative HPT scoring composites, different ideology operationalizations, exclusion of high social desirability responders, random-slope models, and school fixed-effects specifications (all *p*s > .43). These substantive findings complement the measurement evidence: the instrument not only measures equivalently across ideology groups (DIF, invariance) but also yields scores that are uncontaminated by political attitudes at the composite level.

## 5. Discussion

### 5.1 Summary of Findings

This study provides the first psychometric evaluation of a Czech adaptation of the Hartmann and Hasselhorn HPT instrument and the first investigation of ideological fairness for any HPT measure. The Czech adaptation demonstrated the theorized three-factor structure with excellent model fit, acceptable reliability for three-item subscales, meaningful convergent validity with historical knowledge, and sensitivity to school-level variation. DIF analysis revealed no biased items, and multi-group CFA demonstrated full scalar measurement invariance across ideology groups. Historical knowledge was the only construct that predicted HPT scores; political ideology, authoritarianism, and social desirability were all unrelated.

### 5.2 Factor Structure Confirms the Three-Level HPT Model

The CFA results provide strong structural validity evidence for the Czech adaptation. The three-factor model (POP, ROA, CONT) fit the data substantially better than the two-factor and one-factor alternatives, confirming that the Czech version preserves the dimensional structure of the original German instrument. This finding is noteworthy because cross-cultural adaptations do not always replicate the original factor structure [@Hambleton2005; @ITC2017], particularly for instruments measuring complex constructs that may be culturally contingent. The replication of the three-factor HPT structure in a linguistically and educationally distinct context strengthens the claim that Hartmann and Hasselhorn's developmental model captures a generalizable feature of historical reasoning rather than a culture-specific pattern.

The factor loadings (Table 2) reveal meaningful variation within subscales. CONT items loaded most consistently (.61--.72), suggesting that contextualisation items capture a relatively homogeneous construct. POP and ROA subscales each contained one item with a notably weaker loading (.30 for POP2 and ROA2), indicating that not all items within these subscales contribute equally to the factor. These weaker items were retained to preserve comparability with the original instrument, but future adaptation efforts might consider item revision or replacement to strengthen subscale homogeneity.

### 5.3 Addressing Modest Reliability

The reliability values observed in this study ($\alpha$ = .47--.64; $\omega$ = .55--.69) warrant direct discussion, as they fall below conventional benchmarks of .70 or .80 often cited in applied settings. Three points are essential for interpreting these values.

First, with only three items per subscale, reliability coefficients are mathematically constrained. The relationship between test length and reliability is well established: $\alpha$ is a function of both the average inter-item correlation and the number of items. With *k* = 3, even moderate inter-item correlations (around .25--.35) will produce $\alpha$ values in the .47--.64 range. This is a design feature of the instrument, not a deficiency of the Czech adaptation. Any adaptation that faithfully preserves the original three-item-per-subscale structure will face the same constraint.

Second, McDonald's $\omega$ is the more appropriate reliability estimate for these subscales because the items have unequal factor loadings (Table 2), violating the tau-equivalence assumption that $\alpha$ requires. The $\omega$ values (.55--.69) are consistently higher and provide a more accurate picture of the proportion of variance attributable to the latent factor.

Third, the practical consequences of modest reliability must be evaluated against the instrument's demonstrated validity. Despite reliability in the .47--.69 range, the instrument detected the theoretically expected knowledge--HPT relationship ($r$ = .22--.34) and discriminated between HPT levels in the expected developmental ordering (POP_rev > ROA > CONT). If reliability were genuinely inadequate, these valid relationships would be attenuated to the point of non-detection. The fact that they are clearly present demonstrates that the instrument carries sufficient psychometric signal for its intended purposes. More practically, correcting the observed ideology--HPT correlation for attenuation yielded a disattenuated *r* of -.065---indicating that even with perfect reliability, no meaningful ideology association would emerge.

For applied users, we recommend that the HPT instrument be used primarily at the composite level (HPT_CTX6 or HPT_TOT9) rather than interpreting individual subscale scores, given that composite reliability benefits from greater test length.

### 5.4 Full Scalar Invariance Across Ideology Groups

The measurement invariance findings are the central contribution of this study. Full scalar invariance---confirmed through both the composite ideology grouping and the supplementary NS-only analysis---means that the HPT instrument imposes epistemically equivalent measurement demands on students regardless of their political attitudes. The factor structure is the same (configural), the items relate to the factors in the same way (metric), and the item thresholds are the same (scalar) for students with low and high ideological attitudes.

This finding has three implications for score interpretation. First, any observed score differences between ideology groups can be attributed to true differences in HPT competence rather than to measurement bias. Second, the absence of score differences between groups (reported in the companion paper) reflects genuine equivalence in HPT performance, not merely an artifact of compensating item biases. Third, the instrument can be used with confidence in ideologically diverse classroom settings without concern that political attitudes systematically advantage or disadvantage particular students.

The present fairness evidence applies to mainstream Czech adolescent samples with restricted ideological range. Users in contexts with greater political polarisation---post-conflict societies, politically divided classrooms---should conduct their own DIF and invariance analyses before assuming equivalent measurement.

### 5.5 No Differential Item Functioning

The DIF analysis provides item-level evidence complementing the scale-level invariance findings. No item exhibited either uniform or non-uniform DIF, meaning that item discrimination parameters and threshold parameters were statistically equivalent across ideology groups. This is particularly important for the CONT items, which require students to contextualize a historical figure's attraction to political extremism and are therefore the items most theoretically susceptible to ideological contamination. The absence of DIF for these items provides initial evidence that contextualisation, as captured by this instrument, reflects historical reasoning rather than ideological sympathy.

### 5.6 Convergent Validity Evidence

The pattern of correlations provides convergent and discriminant validity evidence consistent with the theoretical conceptualization of HPT. The positive association between historical knowledge and HPT supports the view that perspective taking is a knowledge-dependent competence [@Hartmann2008; @Chapman2021; @Wineburg2001]: students who knew more about the relevant historical period produced more sophisticated responses. Simultaneously, the null correlations with ideology and social desirability indicate that HPT scores are not contaminated by political attitudes or impression management. From a validity framework perspective [@Messick1995; @AERA2014], this pattern---expected correlations present, unwanted correlations absent---constitutes convergent and discriminant evidence for the intended score interpretation.

### 5.7 Implications for Assessment Practice

The findings carry practical implications for educational assessment in domains that involve politically, morally, or culturally sensitive content. The core practical message is that assessment designers should not assume that politically charged content automatically introduces construct-irrelevant variance. Under the conditions examined here---a structured scenario with contextual scaffolding, Likert-scaled items requiring agreement or disagreement, and a mainstream student sample---the HPT instrument functioned with full measurement equivalence across ideology groups.

This does not mean that all instruments using politically sensitive content will be similarly robust. Rather, it provides an evidence-based model for how to evaluate fairness when assessment content intersects with respondents' attitudes. The evaluation protocol used here---CFA for structural validity, DIF for item-level fairness, MG-CFA for scale-level invariance, and convergent/discriminant correlations for nomological evidence---can be applied whenever there is a theoretical reason to suspect that content characteristics might introduce systematic bias.

The consequences of using HPT scores in educational contexts---whether for formative feedback, curricular evaluation, or comparative research---warrant careful consideration, particularly if the instrument is deployed in high-stakes settings for which it was not designed.

### 5.8 Design Features That May Reduce Attitudinal Contamination: Hypotheses for Future Research

Why might the HPT instrument be resistant to ideological contamination? Several features of its design may contribute. First, the scenario provides extensive contextual information before students respond, potentially channeling deliberative processing that overrides attitude-retrieval pathways [@Evans2013]. Second, the Likert items require students to evaluate propositions about a historical figure's reasoning rather than to express their own attitudes directly, creating psychological distance between the respondent's beliefs and the response task. Third, the assessment context (a school-administered instrument) may activate an academic frame that further separates personal attitudes from task-oriented cognition.

These features represent testable hypotheses about what protects assessments from attitudinal contamination, warranting empirical verification across varied instruments and contexts. Assessment designers working with historically, politically, or ethically charged content can draw on these hypotheses while recognizing that empirical verification---through the kind of fairness evaluation reported here---remains essential for each new instrument and context.

### 5.9 Limitations

Several limitations warrant discussion.

*Limitation 1: Single scenario and country.* The study uses a single country (Czech Republic) and a single scenario (Weimar Republic). Whether the Czech adaptation functions similarly with different historical scenarios, and whether the fairness findings generalize to other national contexts where the Weimar content carries different resonances, remains to be established.

*Limitation 2: Restricted ideological range.* FR-LF scores were concentrated below the scale midpoint (only 17.7% above the midpoint of 3.0), and even within the High ideology tertile (*n* = 96), only 50% exceeded the midpoint. The DIF and invariance analyses thus compare moderately elevated to low ideology, not genuine political extremism. The findings should be understood as evidence of instrument fairness under mainstream classroom conditions, not as a guarantee of fairness under conditions of genuine ideological extremism.

*Limitation 3: Modest subscale reliability.* HPT subscale reliability is modest ($\alpha$ = .47--.64). While we have argued that this is structurally constrained by the three-item design and that the instrument demonstrates valid predictor relationships despite this limitation, higher reliability would strengthen individual-level score interpretations. Future adaptations should consider expanding subscale length.

*Limitation 4: DIF power.* The sample (*N* = 285--293 across 10 schools) provides adequate power for moderate effects but limited sensitivity for DIF analysis specifically, where *n* $\approx$ 96 per group limits detection of weak item-level differential functioning. With *n* $\approx$ 96 per group and a four-category graded response model (three threshold parameters per item), the LRT-based DIF analysis was powered primarily to detect moderate-to-large DIF effects. Post hoc power estimation for polytomous DIF with these sample sizes suggests sensitivity to uniform DIF of approximately $\Delta\beta$ $\geq$ 0.5 logits at 80% power. Weak DIF effects ($\Delta\beta$ < 0.3 logits) cannot be confidently excluded. Larger samples would enable more precise DIF estimation and permit testing of additional invariance levels (e.g., strict invariance).

*Limitation 5: Common method variance.* All measures were self-report instruments administered in a single session, introducing common method variance as a potential concern. The absence of social desirability effects provides partial reassurance, but multi-method designs would strengthen validity evidence.

*Limitation 6: Closed-ended format.* The closed-ended Likert format may not generalize to open-ended HPT assessments where ideology could operate through different mechanisms such as argument framing or source selection [@Smith2017; @Smith2018].

*Limitation 7: No response process evidence.* The present study provides evidence of internal structure (CFA) and relations to other variables (convergent validity) but lacks response process evidence---cognitive interviews or think-aloud data showing that students engage disciplinary reasoning when responding to HPT items. The absence of a cognitive pilot study (e.g., think-aloud interviews) means that response process validity evidence is not available for the Czech adaptation. This remains an important gap in the validity argument that future research should address. Future work should include cognitive interviewing to verify that Czech students engage disciplinary reasoning processes when responding to HPT items.

### 5.10 Recommendations for Future HPT Instrument Development

Based on the present findings, we offer several recommendations for researchers developing or adapting HPT instruments.

1. *Report subscale-level reliability alongside composite reliability.* Three-item subscales will inevitably show modest $\alpha$; reporting $\omega$ and providing the Spearman-Brown projected values for longer tests contextualizes these coefficients appropriately.

2. *Evaluate fairness with respect to theoretically relevant grouping variables.* For instruments using historically or politically sensitive content, DIF and measurement invariance analyses across relevant attitude groups should be standard practice, not an afterthought.

3. *Consider item revision for weak-loading items.* Items with loadings at or near .30 (POP2, ROA2 in the present study) contribute minimally to their factors. Future versions might replace or revise these items while preserving the theoretical structure.

4. *Expand subscale length if psychometric precision is a priority.* Moving from three to five or six items per subscale would substantially improve reliability while preserving the three-factor theoretical structure.

5. *Test fairness across diverse samples.* The present study provides fairness evidence for mainstream Czech classrooms. Replications in contexts with greater ideological polarization, different historical scenarios, and diverse cultural backgrounds would map the boundary conditions of instrument fairness.

### 5.11 Conclusion

This study provides initial evidence that the Czech adaptation of the Hartmann and Hasselhorn HPT instrument preserves the three-factor structure of the original, produces scores that relate to historical knowledge in theoretically expected ways, and functions with full measurement equivalence across students with different political attitudes. The absence of DIF and the achievement of scalar measurement invariance support the conclusion that the politically charged scenario content does not introduce construct-irrelevant variance in mainstream classroom settings. For the measurement community, the key finding is that structured scenario-based assessments can employ sensitive historical content without compromising fairness---provided that the instrument design channels disciplinary reasoning rather than attitudinal expression, and provided that fairness is verified empirically rather than assumed. The Czech HPT adaptation is now available for use in comparative research on historical reasoning, with the psychometric evidence reported here supporting its use in Czech educational contexts and its potential as a component of cross-national validation studies.

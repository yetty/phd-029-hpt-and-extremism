I am writing to submit the manuscript entitled **"Does Identity-Protective Cognition Operate in Educational Assessment? Political Attitudes and Historical Reasoning in Czech Adolescents"** for consideration as an original research article in *Social Psychology of Education*.

## Scope and Fit

The manuscript investigates whether identity-protective cognition---a well-established phenomenon in social psychology---extends into educational assessment contexts. Specifically, we tested whether Czech adolescents' political attitudes bias their performance on a historical perspective-taking task involving politically charged content. This research sits squarely within SPE's scope by bridging social psychological theory (motivated reasoning, identity-protective cognition, dual-process models) with educational measurement concerns (assessment fairness, construct validity in ideologically diverse classrooms).

## Key Findings

Using multilevel models and equivalence testing with 293 secondary students across 10 schools, we found no association between right-authoritarian attitudes and historical reasoning performance. Two One-Sided Tests (TOST) provided positive evidence that ideology effects are practically equivalent to zero. Historical knowledge was the sole consistent predictor. An exploratory analysis revealed a small association between authoritarianism and presentism, consistent with a rigidity pathway rather than identity-protective cognition. These findings identify a boundary condition for motivated reasoning theory and offer practical implications for assessment design in politically sensitive domains.
